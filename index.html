What are they?

Japanese Quail are a species of game bird in the pheasant family. Frequently kept for meat and eggs, these diminutive birds are incredibly versatile (and CUTE!). They have been kept since at least the 12th century as domesticated animals (according to Wikipedia). Quail are very easy to care for, as you will see. They will live happily in a rabbit hutch or aviary. Some people even keep them in cages, however I personally feel more space is better for birds, even tiny ones like these!

How do I care for my birds?

As with any pet, birds have needs, and quail are no different! They need somewhere clean to live, I highly reccomend a good hutch setup. they need food in the form of game bird feed, ideally with a higher protein level. They should ideally also be given leafy greens regularly, alongside some kind of grit that is rich in calcium.

If you are intending to breed your birds then it is usually a good idea to keep 1 male or "roo", to every 3 to 5 female "hens". You should usually be able to collect 1 egg per day per hen, which can then be put in an incubator for hatching or in the fridge for eating. Some people dont like the idea of eating fertilised eggs, but from my experience there really isn't alot of difference.


Hatching your eggs!

Incubation

You can buy or even build your own incubator fairly easily. The eggs need to be turned regularly to help prevent the developing chick from sticking to the inside of the shell. Japanese quail eggs require a temperature of 37.5 degrees C, or 99.5 degrees if you work in farenheit, in order to incubate and develop properly. You should cease turning eggs after around day 14, this is known as lockdown, as far as i can tell this is because the now nearly fully developed chick is not at risk of sticking to the inside of the egg.



Into the brooder

Once hatched, quail chicks can remain in the incubator for upto 24 hours. They can be safely removed as soon as their fuzzy chick down is dry, and from then on need to be put in a larger enclosure called a "brooder". A brooder is designed to keep the chicks warm until their feathers have fully developed, as until that point they cannot regulate their body temperatures by themselves. The brooder needs a floor covered with material that provides good traction for the chicks, as "splay leg" can be a real issue.

Splay leg is a common health condition in baby quail, it can be brought on by a wide range of different things including:

  • Incorrect incubating perameters
  • Slipping and sliding on the incubator or brooder floor
  • Improper nutrition in the parents
Whilst very common, it is also very treatable especially if caught early. Whatever the underlying reason for the condition, the problem is usually strain or damage to the muscles in the chicks hips. Keeping their legs underneath them and the chick immobile usually does the trick for me. I have achieved this by putting the little bird inside a shot glass near, but not too near, the heat source. I usually do this overnight.

There are a few ways to heat a brooder, most commonly with a red heat lamp. I say a red lamp specifically as a full spectrum white lamp will make it difficult for the chicks to sleep at night, which is not a good thing. I have also had some success with using a reptile heat mat beneath a layer of tissue paper. There are also "fake" hens available, which provide heat from above just like a real mother would.
